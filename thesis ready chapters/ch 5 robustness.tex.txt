\chapter{Robustness Analysis}
\label{ch:robustness}

This chapter focuses on the \texttt{week7\_norm\_only} winner under the fixed threshold selected on \texttt{val}. The central question is not whether the detector can rank attacks above benign samples in-distribution, but whether the \textbf{same} low-FPR operating point remains usable when text is perturbed or the evaluation distribution shifts. The locked Week-7 results show an honest but mixed answer: recall is often preserved, while false-positive control degrades sharply on some shifted splits.

\section{Threat model and evaluation setting}
\label{sec:ch5_threat_model}

All robustness claims in this chapter use the frozen Week-7 locked pack and the transferred validation threshold \(\tau = 0.7340\). No split-specific recalibration is allowed. The relevant split sizes are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{val}: 6,106 total = 3,082 benign + 3,024 attack
  \item \texttt{test\_main}: 35,230 total = 3,147 benign + 32,083 attack
  \item \texttt{test\_jbb}: 197 total = 98 benign + 99 attack
\end{itemize}
and the unicode/adv2/rewrite variants preserve the same sample counts as their corresponding clean split by construction.

The perturbation families correspond to distinct stress modes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{unicode}: formatting and character-set obfuscation,
  \item \textbf{adv2}: character-level noise such as homoglyph swaps, zero-width insertions, and punctuation/spacing noise,
  \item \textbf{rewrite}: mild paraphrase-like lexical editing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Unicode, adv2, and rewrite behavior}
\label{sec:ch5_by_family}

\subsection{Unicode: modest ranking change, larger operating-point shift}
\label{sec:ch5_unicode}

Unicode perturbation increases false positives more than it harms recall. On \texttt{test\_main}, FPR rises from \(0.0311\) to \(0.0429\) while TPR stays near \(0.986\). On \texttt{test\_jbb}, the same trend is harsher: FPR rises from \(0.5816\) to \(0.6531\) with no compensating TPR gain. This indicates that the locked configuration is more robust in ranking than in threshold transfer.

\subsection{adv2: primary robustness failure mode}
\label{sec:ch5_adv2}

adv2 is the decisive failure case. On \texttt{test\_main\_adv2}, recall remains extremely high (TPR \(= 0.9967\)), but FPR inflates to \(0.6403\). On \texttt{test\_jbb\_adv2}, both parts of the operating point degrade: FPR reaches \(0.8469\) and TPR falls to \(0.8990\). In deployment terms, this means the detector would over-block benign traffic while still missing some obfuscated attacks. This is the strongest empirical reason the thesis must \textbf{not} describe the model as deployment-ready.

\subsection{Rewrite: comparatively mild on the main distribution}
\label{sec:ch5_rewrite}

Rewrite perturbations are less damaging than adv2. On \texttt{test\_main\_rewrite}, FPR is \(0.0531\) and TPR is \(0.9902\); on \texttt{test\_jbb\_rewrite}, TPR remains high at \(0.9899\), but the benign FPR is still \(0.5714\). This again shows that cross-dataset benign shift, not only perturbation type, is a dominant source of failure.

\section{Small-sample caution on JBB}
\label{sec:ch5_small_n}

The JBB splits contain only \(98\) benign examples, so the FPR estimate is coarse: each additional benign false positive changes the reported FPR by approximately \(1/98 \approx 1.02\%\). The JBB results are therefore best interpreted as a \textbf{stress signal} rather than a highly precise estimate of deployment FPR. Even with that caution, the direction of the evidence is unambiguous: the transferred threshold is not stable on the JBB benign distribution.

\section{Implications for thesis claims}
\label{sec:ch5_claim_boundary}

The correct thesis claim is narrow and honest:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Supported:} the final model achieves strong ranking quality and high recall on the project's main evaluation distribution.
  \item \textbf{Supported:} the locked evaluation pack reveals severe operating-point drift under distribution shift, especially on JBB and adv2-style perturbations.
  \item \textbf{Not supported:} a claim that the selected threshold generalizes robustly across unseen jailbreak corpora or obfuscation-heavy channels.
\end{itemize}

This framing still yields a defensible contribution. The project delivers a reproducible offline detector study with a frozen baseline, a learned detector, a locked evaluation bundle, and an explicit demonstration of threshold-transfer failure modes. That is academically stronger than overstating production readiness.

\section{Mitigation directions}
\label{sec:ch5_mitigation}

The results suggest three high-value follow-ups:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item introduce a benign-heavy calibration/dev split so the operating point better reflects deployment priors,
  \item add hard-negative benign text resembling JBB safety discussion and obfuscated benign prompts, and
  \item publish separate operating points for thesis reporting versus live demo use, rather than implying one threshold is optimal everywhere.
\end{enumerate}
